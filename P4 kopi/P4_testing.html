We asked the testers general questions about the site - how it looked, how easy or hard the navigation was and the general usability of the site. We also gave them specific tasks where they had to find a particular part of the website and grade the difficulty of the task.

Tester Audience

We invited the members on the official Music Technologies Facebook page to drop by Fjordgata to test our new website, meaning that 100% of the test group was college students. We decided to both test the page on single individuals and groups of 3. This made us able to simulate different scenarios in which people need to access the page. In total, this resulted in 17 test groups. Most of the testing was done live, where we saw their reactions, asked them to fill out the Google form, and then come with verbal feedback for anything that was not covered by the form.

Testing Platforms

The website was tested on the following devices

  • MacBook Pro, both 13" and 15"
  • iMac

The website was tested on the following browsers:

  • Google Chrome

  • Opera

Feedback Summary

Overall our testers were very positive to the general design, stating that it was a clean and easy design that was easy to navigate. However, some of them noted that the "section spacing" had some inconsistencies. We also took note of the fact that many of our testers did not check out the slide show-functionality. As far as we could tell, this was because the functionality didn't catch their attention at first (/we pointed it out before they left). We were also not quite satisfied with the time it took before the testers realised how to navigate the site, which we hoped would be in less than 5 seconds after seeing the front page.

Our Response to the Feedback

Given the feedback we got, we would try to further mark the transitions between the sections. We are also thinking of implementing buttons at the bottom of the home page which directs the user to the different sections in the same way that the nav bar does, as we think this would be an effective way to introduce first time viewers to the possibility to scroll through the whole website.

Self-Reflections

What we learned most of all was the importance of inputs from testers. Given that we were to do it all over again, getting a beta version out for testing ASAP would be our top priority - the inputs we got really cut down the necessary time on polishing-phase, and we suspect it would do the same in earlier phases as well. We also found that some of our javascript ideas were already available in CSS, and implementing them in CSS made the web page more responsive overall. When it comes to designing we got a better grasp of what "minimalistic design" really means - for the lack of a better way to describe it: It's just as much work in deciding how to present what little content there is as in deciding what to omit.
